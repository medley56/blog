# Theory of Mind

With all the recent buzz surrounding large language models (LLMs) and AI generally, 
I've been doing a lot of armchair thinking about how people think. Specifically I've 
been focusing on how thoughts come together and how our brains generate original thoughts. 
LLMs, and ChatGPT4 in particular, have recently producing responses that I genuinely cannot 
distinguish from a human response -- for me, GPT4 is pretty much passing the Turing Test. 
This leads me to wonder whether we are modeling human thought using a crude approximation 
that manages to deliver human-like results or whether we have accidentally fallen into  
an Occam's Razor moment and these LLMs are really generating responses in the same way 
as we do. If so, humanity might once again have to face the likelihood that the meat computers 
in our heads are really not as special as we have long assumed.

When experts talk about LLMs, they usually describe the process of generating responses as 
linking together tokenized representations of text. That is, the learning data has been broken 
down not into words or sentences, but into partial words or even single characters. The LLM 
simply chooses the most likely token to follow the current chunk of text. Obviously there's 
more to it than that when it comes to things like context-awareness, safety measures, and 
other additional controls that most companies are putting in place but that's the general idea. 
On the other hand, when we humans think we don't perceive our thought process as being nearly so 
linear nor so simple. Ideas seem to simply come to us out of the blue. When we try to remember 
something, sometimes we can bring that data to our conscious mind seemingly through sheer force 
of will. It feels like our brains work in a much more sophisticated manner than an LLM. 

But what if that's all just human exceptionalism? 

Humans are always assuming that we are special in the universe. Tool use, language, and sentience itself 
were once thought to be traits exclusive to humans. However, as we learn more about the animal kingdom 
we consistently learn that these supposedly unique traits are widespread across pretty much all of life. 
Additionally, the more we fill in our gaps of knowledge about how these traits are distributed, the more 
it appears that sentience is not really a boolean trait that is either "on" or "off" but rather that 
all life is on a continuum of sentience and that by some measure (our measure), we are the _most_ 
sentient. There are relatively recent 
[findings about language in prairie dog colonies](https://jan.ucc.nau.edu/~cns3/) 
and the ability of plants to 
respond to stimuli with a sort of 
[proto nervous system](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pmc/articles/PMC5603676/) 
that uses action potentials but lacks true neurons. 
Given that, I am hard pressed to see humans as anything but a 
difference in degree of sentience rather than in kind. Indeed, based on what we know about how selection 
pressures act on populations of genes, it seems absolutely ridiculous to think that sentience arose 
all at once in humans rather than slowly over time and in varying degrees, conferring varying degrees 
of evolutionary advantage. 

I now wonder if we are making 
the same mistake when we talk about how our brains work. Is there anything fundamentally different about 
a human brians versus that of a monkey? A rat? A lizard? I doubt it. Ours are bigger and have evolved to 
solve a different set of evolutionary challenges but I don't see a reason to assume that our fundamental 
mechanism for thinking should be any different. The chemistry that causes neurons to fire is actually 
[pretty basic at its core](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Action_potential); 
ionic gradients produce a resting potential of about -70mV across the neuron's 
cell membrane. When upstream neurons fire, they release neurotransmitters, which alter the permeability 
of the downstream neuron to sodium and calcium ions. If enough sodium and calcium ions are allowed into 
the downstream neuron as a result of neurotransmitters opening channels, the change inside that neuron 
moves closer to 0, which creates a temporary positive feedback loop, allowing more and more positive ions 
inside, which causes the neuron to release its own neurotransmitters, affecting all the downstream neurons. 
This mechanism works the same way in pretty much all life that has neurons, 
[from simple rotifers to humans](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/List_of_animals_by_number_of_neurons). 

With that image in mind, I don't think it's a huge leap to imagine that neuron firing and token selection 
are really not so different. Perhaps our LLMs are approximating true thought much better than we initially 
believed. It's a stretch to say that we have somehow landed on the precise mechanism for thought generation 
but it's completely believable that we have gotten close enough to imperfectly create genuine thoughts using 
machines. After all, the process of thinking is inherently very robust to perturbation. Killing off a few 
neurons (or tens of thousands) doesn't really affect our brains much, nor neurons always fire in a particular 
order or volume. The entire system of our brains is extremely resilient, indicating that there is probably 
a lot of wiggle room for things to occur differently while retaining the overall function of thinking.

As I said in my preamble, I'm not an expert on this stuff but I like the exercise of generalizing and
